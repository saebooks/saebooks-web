Why this matters
If MYR were missed, the deemed-dividend amount under s.109D would be the lesser of (a) the unpaid amount and (b) the distributable surplus. With A$365,741 of headroom, any non-compliance event would crystallise at full value — meaning grossed-up A$522,487 in shareholder assessable income at 47% top MTR = A$245k extra tax. The compliance discipline pays for itself.
Note on aggregating amounts
s.109Y(3) aggregation rule: surplus is reduced by amounts deemed paid as dividends to all entities in same income year. Currently nil — LN-2603 is the only Div 7A loan SAE has.
Franking position
If a deemed dividend ever triggers, the company may frank it under s.109RC. SAE franking-account balance A$84,261 (after FY26 PAYG instalments) provides ~A$196k of frankable headroom at 30% rate — still well short of distributable surplus. Don't rely on franking to soften a Div 7A failure.

Bendel outcome
Not a Div 7A "loan"

The Full Federal Court confirmed (Feb 2025) that an unpaid present entitlement owed by a trust to a corporate beneficiary is not a "loan" under s.109D(3) ITAA 1936.

Result: pre-existing UPEs and new ones do not, in themselves, trigger deemed dividends.

ATO Decision Impact Statement (12 Mar 2025) confirms ATO position superseded — TD 2022/11 and PCG 2017/13 withdrawn / under review.

SAE practical position
No sub-trust required

UPE balance A$140,522 sits as a present entitlement on the trust balance sheet. No s.109N agreement is required.

Watch points: (a) any actual loan-back from SAE to the trust or its beneficiaries remains a Div 7A loan; (b) ATO has flagged possible legislative response — monitor MYEFO 2026.

Treasury Laws Amendment (Tax Integrity) 2025 currently exposure draft — not yet enacted; would re-characterise UPE as loan from 1 Jul 2027 if passed.
